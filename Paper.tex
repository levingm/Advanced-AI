\subsection{Problem Setting and Data}
% - Target variable definition -> CHECK if we want to use EINLAGEVOLUMEN?
The target variable $\mathrm{Einlagevolumen}$is monthly end-of-period levels of overnight deposits held by private households, published by the Deutsche Bundesbank \citep{bundesbank_data}. The dataset covers the period January 2003 to December 2025. Six covariates are used to enrich the data set.\\ 
% - Data source(s), time period, granularity MUST CHECK REFS
The Euro Short-Term Rate ($\text{€STR}$), sourced from the ECB
\citep{ecb_estr}, is available at daily frequency and captures
the average overnight borrowing rate of banks in the euro area.
As each published value reflects the previous business day's
transactions, it constitutes a direct, transaction-based measure
of interbank market conditions. While $\text{€STR}$ was
officially introduced in October 2019, the sample period extends
back to January 2003. For the period March 2017 to September
2019, the Pre-$\text{€STR}$ \citep{ecb_preestr} is used as a
direct predecessor series. For earlier observations, the series
is reconstructed following \citet[Ch.~5.2]{winkler2025}.
The German geopolitical risk index ($\mathrm{GPRC\_DEU}$) published on a monthly basis \citep{gpr_index}, quantifies the geopolitical risk in Germany by using automated text search in the U.S. newspapers \textit{New York Times}, \textit{Chicago Tribune} and \textit{Washington Post}.
The month-on-month inflation ($\mathrm{MoM-Inflation}$) sourced from Destatis \citep{destatis_inflation}, calculates the
percentage change in the Consumer Price Index relative to the
preceding month.
The DAX daily closing prices are obtained via the yfinance Python library  \citep{yfinance}, retrieving data from Yahoo Finance \citep{yahoo_finance_dax} for the ticker \texttt{\^{}GDAXI}.
The 10-year German government bond yield ($\mathrm{10Y~Bond}$),
sourced at business-day frequency from the Deutsche Bundesbank
\citep{bundesbank_bonds}. 
% - Preprocessing steps
\paragraph{Interpolation Methods}
Since multiple variables are published at monthly frequency, they therefore require interpolation to a daily grid before model training. 
Following \citet[Ch.~5.2]{winkler2025}, piecewise cubic Hermite interpolating polynomial (PCHIP) interpolation—introduced by \cite{fritsch_carlson}—outperforms than linear and autoregressive methods for financial time series.
PCHIP interpolation is applied to $\mathrm{GPRC\_DEU}$ and
$\mathrm{MoM~Inflation}$, preserving local monotonicity of the input series and avoiding artificial overshoots between monthly observations \citep{fritsch_carlson}.
The $\mathrm{Einlagevolumen}$ and $\mathrm{Einlagezinssatz}$
series, as well as the three-period $\text{€STR}$ reconstruction
described above, are constructed following
the methodology of \citet[Ch.~5.1--5.2]{winkler2025}. The DAX
closing prices and the 10-year bond yield are natively available
at daily frequency and require no interpolation.
For variables available at business-day frequency
($\text{€STR}$ and $\mathrm{10Y~Bond}$), weekend and public
holiday gaps are handled via forward-fill, carrying the last
observed value forward to the following business day. This
ensures a complete daily index across the full sample period
while avoiding the introduction of artificial variation on
non-trading days. The following figure illustrates illustrates all variables over the full sample period after interpolation to daily frequency.
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/overview.png}

As evident from Figure~\ref{fig:timeseries_overview}, the $\mathrm{Einlagevolumen}$ exhibits a clear upward trend throughout the timeline, indicating nonstationarity in the mean. To this first differencing is applied, to obtain a stationary target series and to limit accumulating directional errors \citep{box2015},
as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:diff_volume}. Second differencing was rejected do to over-differencing.

After interpolation to daily frequency, the dataset comprises
1517 observations covering the full sample period January
2003 to December 2025.

\subsection{Models}
\label{subsec:models}
All models produce a $\mathrm{PROGNOSEHORIZONT} = 100$-step-ahead
forecast of $\Delta y_t$ from a $\mathrm{GEDAECHTNIS} = 30$-day lookback
window, using only information available up to the training cutoff
(no future covariate values are used as input). Two naive benchmarks
and three ``sequence'' models share this forecasting setup; a
Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) is evaluated separately due to its
distinct handling of known vs.\ unknown future covariates. The models
appear in order of increasing complexity, from fully
interpretable benchmarks to black-box architectures. This allows an
evaluation of whether additional model complexity translates into
forecasting gains.

\paragraph{Naive benchmarks.}
Random Walk extrapolates the last observed daily change at a constant
rate,
\begin{equation}
    \hat{y}_{t_0+h} = y_{t_0} + h \cdot \Delta y_{t_0}, \qquad h = 1, \dots, 100,
    \label{eq:naive_rw}
\end{equation}
which corresponds to an ARIMA$(0,1,0)$ process with drift
\citep[Ch.~4.1.2]{box2015}. Naive Hold instead assumes the level
remains constant, $\hat{y}_{t_0+h} = y_{t_0}$. Both benchmarks are
fully interpretable, require no covariates, serving as a lower
bound against which the higher complexity of the remaining models
must be justified.

\paragraph{Linear Regression (Ridge).}
A multi-output ridge regression maps the flattened lookback window
(target and covariates, optionally including lags) to the full
100-step \\CHECK\\ forecast vector directly (a ``direct'' rather than
``iterated'' multi-step strategy), regularized by an $\ell_2$
penalty $\alpha$ selected via time-series cross-validation
\citep[Ch.~3]{hastie2009}. As a linear model, Ridge retains full
interpretability of covariate effects through its coefficients,
at the cost of being unable to capture non-linear relationships
between covariates and deposit behavior.

\paragraph{XGBoost.}
Gradient-boosted regression trees are trained on the same flattened
input representation, with hyperparameters (max depth, learning rate,
subsample ratio) selected via grid search under
\texttt{TimeSeriesSplit} cross-validation to avoid look-ahead bias in
model selection \citep{chen2016}. Boosted trees can capture
non-linear covariate interactions that Ridge cannot
\citep[Ch.~10]{hastie2009}, while remaining moderately interpretable
through feature importance measures -- positioning XGBoost as an
intermediate step between the fully interpretable linear model and
the black-box neural architectures that follow.

\paragraph{Feedforward Neural Network (FFN).}
A fully connected network with two hidden layers (128, 64 units,
ReLU activation) is trained to directly predict the 100-step output
vector. Following the general formulation of a feedforward layer
\citep[Ch.~11]{hastie2009} (cf.\ also \citet[Ch.~4.2]{winkler2025}),
\begin{equation}
    z_j = \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_{ji} x_i + b_j, \qquad a_j = \phi(z_j),
    \label{eq:ffn_neuron}
\end{equation}
where $\phi$ is the ReLU activation for hidden layers. Training uses
Adam with early stopping on a held-out validation slice (last 15\% of
the training window, chronologically), monitoring validation MAE with
a patience of 20 iterations. Unlike Ridge and XGBoost, the FFN offers
no direct interpretability of individual covariate effects, trading
transparency for greater representational flexibility.

\paragraph{Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT).}
Unlike the sequence models above, the TFT explicitly distinguishes
between \emph{known} future inputs (deterministically known for the
entire forecast horizon -- here, calendar features: weekday,
month-end indicator, day-of-month) and \emph{unknown} future inputs
(the six macro-financial covariates, whose future values are not
known and are therefore held at their last observed value throughout
the forecast horizon, consistent with the realistic no-future-leakage
scenario described in Section~\ref{subsec:data}) \citep{lim2021}.
The model is trained with early stopping (patience 5 epochs) on
validation loss. Despite being the most complex model considered,
the TFT's attention mechanism was explicitly designed by
\citet{lim2021} to offer a degree of interpretability -- e.g.\ via
variable selection weights -- that purely black-box architectures
typically lack, distinguishing it from a conventional deep learning
model of comparable complexity.

\paragraph{Ensembling.}
Each sequence model is fit \(S\) times with different random seeds
(and, when \(S>1\), bootstrap-resampled training data) to form an
ensemble; point forecasts are the ensemble mean, and 90\%/98\%
confidence bands are the corresponding empirical percentiles
(5/95 and 1/99) across ensemble paths, following the same
visualization convention as \citet[Ch.~5.4]{winkler2025}.

All models are evaluated under the identical rolling-origin scheme
described below, allowing a direct, like-for-like comparison across
the full interpretability--complexity spectrum.
